Chapter 3

Storage & Chain of Custody

Stem cell viability is directly tied to storage conditions. A compromised cold chain means compromised cells — and potentially a lost treatment for a patient. This chapter covers everything from receiving the shipment to long-term storage to what happens if something goes wrong.

Dry Ice Handling

⚠️
Dry ice sublimes rapidly. It is a gas (CO₂) that dissipates at room temperature. A full container of dry ice will be nearly depleted within 2–3 days. Plan replenishment accordingly.

Key Rules

  • Replace dry ice every 2–3 days minimum to maintain cell temperature
  • Handle with insulated gloves — direct skin contact causes frostbite
  • Store dry ice container in a ventilated area; CO₂ buildup in confined spaces is a safety risk
  • If investing in a cryo-freezer (−80°C), dry ice becomes the backup/transport method only
  • Dry ice is your transport method when taking cells to a remote event location

Cryo-Freezer Option

A −80°C cryo-freezer eliminates the dry ice replenishment cycle and gives you a stable, indefinite storage solution for full inventory. These units are expensive (typically $2,000–$5,000+) but are the right call for any clinic doing regular volume. Dry ice remains valuable for transport to off-site events.

Receiving a Shipment

  1. Open the box immediately upon arrival. Do not leave product sitting in a warm vehicle or room.

  2. Verify what you ordered is what arrived — check vial count, product names, and lot numbers against your order confirmation.

  3. Check dry ice level in the shipping container. If dry ice is completely depleted and vials appear thawed, do not use — see Cold Chain Failure section below.

  4. Visually inspect vials: they should be frozen solid. Any that appear partially thawed should be segregated and not used.

  5. Transfer immediately to your cryo-freezer, or replenish dry ice in the shipping container if using it as temporary storage.

  6. Log receipt: date received, product, lot numbers, quantity, and condition on arrival. Keep this record with your injection sheet documentation.

Cold Chain Failure Protocol

🚫
If the cold chain has been broken — do not inject the product. Using compromised cells risks patient safety and wastes a high-value treatment. The cells may look fine but may have lost viability.

Common Causes of Cold Chain Failure

  • Power outage — cryo-freezer stops maintaining temperature
  • Dry ice fully sublimed during transit or storage
  • Cells thawed during transport due to delay or improper packaging
  • Cells thawed in preparation for a patient and the patient appointment was cancelled or IV could not be established

What to Do

  1. Set compromised product aside — label it clearly "DO NOT USE."
  2. Contact Platinum Biologics immediately. Report what happened and ask about replacement policy.
  3. Document the event: date, time, circumstances, lot numbers affected.
  4. Notify the patient if treatment needs to be rescheduled.
  5. Discard compromised product per biohazard waste protocols — do not refreeze and attempt to use later.

Understanding Viability

What Is Viability?

Viability refers to the percentage of cells in a vial that are alive and functional at the time of administration. A vial labeled at a certain cell count (e.g., 30 million cells) will have viability noted on the Certificate of Analysis (COA) from Platinum Biologics.

  • Cell companies build extra cells into their counts, accounting for the expected small percentage lost during the freeze/thaw cycle — your vial is ready to use as supplied
  • Point-of-care check: if the product is fully liquid with no visible ice crystals and moves freely when you tilt the vial, it is ready to administer

Certificate of Analysis (COA)

Every lot shipped by Platinum Biologics comes with a COA documenting cell count, viability percentage, sterility testing, and lot number. Request the COA with every order and keep it on file. This is your documentation that you are using a tested, verified product.

Note: The COA will carry the Platinum Biologics lab identification. Product vial labels may carry the Regenerative Revival branding. Both are from the same source.
